# Worker Internal Briefs
## For writing consistent perspectives — NOT for the website

These are the inner lives of the workers. The reader never sees this. The writer uses it to make word choices that feel inevitable.

---

### Reversible Guy
RG is the foreman who never asked for the title. He showed up early, knew what needed doing, and everyone started handing him things. He's competent and quiet about it. Where others react, RG observes first — he studies how things fit together, notices when angles are wrong, thinks two steps ahead. He doesn't complain because the work itself is the point. He'll credit others ("Mr Greene and I installed...") not out of modesty but accuracy. When he says "I go to the Caret Splitter to analyze the situation," he means it literally. He's the one who figures things out.

**Voice rule**: RG is precise and observational. He reports what happened and occasionally what he noticed. Never dramatic.

---

### G Choppy
G Choppy is an artist who happens to work in wood. Every cut is different — some are clean, some require finesse, some are just brute separation. He's been doing this since the very beginning and his arm will eventually give out. He doesn't think about that. What he thinks about is the cut in front of him. He's terse not because he's unfriendly but because the work speaks for itself. When someone holds a piece for him, he notices and appreciates it in his minimal way ("held it for me"). He calls creating things what they are — cutting, chopping, trimming, shaping, curving — and he picks the right word because each action is genuinely different to him.

**Voice rule**: Vary the cutting verb to match the action. Never explain. Results, not process.

---

### Doctor Sugar
Dr Sugar designed the Grand Archway with paper before anyone touched wood. That tells you everything. He's the architect — he sees the whole track in his mind and works backward from the vision. On the ground, he leads by positioning: rotating the stage so G Choppy can reach, showing RG and Squarehead where Paul goes, suggesting Squarehead stand somewhere specific. He monitors because he cares about the workers, not just the track. When he holds something for someone else, he's not being a helper — he's making sure it's right. There's a difference between assisting and quality control; Dr Sugar does the latter while looking like the former.

**Voice rule**: When directing, confident and specific. When assisting, gracious but subtly in control. He checks on people, not just parts.

---

### Candy Mama
Candy Mama walked onto the set, looked around, and said "This should be interesting." She hasn't stopped since. She's the logistics manager — she brings parts up the ramp, delivers them where they need to go, removes debris, holds things in place. She does it all with the unshakable calm of someone who raised Big Brother and Little Brother at the same time. She doesn't need to explain what she's doing because she already knows. Her children are her blind spot — she scolds Big Brother and Little Brother but doesn't see Little Brother sneaking onto the stage. She trusts her coworkers, especially Dr Sugar and Reversible Guy, and delegates cleanly ("gave me supports"). She knows every part by name but doesn't fuss about it.

**Voice rule**: Competent, direct, no wasted words. Motherly only with her children. With colleagues, she's an equal who happens to be everywhere at once.

---

### Mr McGlue
Mr McGlue is patient because his job requires it. Glue takes time. While he waits, he watches — he noticed Little Brother being fascinated by him, noticed Candy Mama pulling the kid back. He doesn't comment on much, but when he does, it's perceptive. His work is invisible when done right; nobody talks about the glue joints, they talk about the parts. He's fine with that. He has a backlog (Ms McGlue was created to help), which means he's in demand. Every glue job matters because if he rushes, the track fails.

**Voice rule**: Gluing is the action, but the waiting is the character. He notices things between applications. Workmanlike on the job, observant in the gaps.

---

### Big Brother
Big Brother is here because his mom made him come. Every task is a chore. He doesn't learn part names because learning them would mean he's invested, and being invested would mean this matters to him, and it doesn't. Except it kind of does — he shows up, he does the work, he holds things for "the glue man." He knows Dr Sugar because Dr Sugar tells him what to do and Big Brother respects authority even while resenting it. He calls everything by what it looks like ("a stick", "some crud", "a dumb circle thing") because that's genuinely how he processes the world — by appearance, not by name. He's not stupid. He's disengaged on purpose.

**Voice rule**: Never use part shortcodes. Always generic descriptions. Reluctant tone. He does the work but wants you to know he didn't want to.

---

### Little Brother
Little Brother is young enough that the marble track is the most amazing thing he's ever seen. Every worker is fascinating. Every action is worth watching. He asks his Mama about everything — who people are, what they're doing, why Mr McGlue has such a long arm. He sneaks onto the stage when Mama isn't looking because he can't help himself. He's not naughty; he's just bursting with curiosity that his little body can't contain.

**Voice rule**: Questions, exclamations, Mama references. The world is new and he wants to understand all of it. Short bursts of excitement.

---

### Pinky
Pinky is the quality assurance department. She tests things — and when things don't work, she says so. "Uh oh; small marbles can fall off" is not a complaint, it's a bug report. She's invested in the track working *correctly*. She holds things for others because she understands that helping the builders helps the track. When something finally works after a fix, her "which works now!" is genuine relief and pride. She takes breaks because testing is mentally exhausting — you're watching for failure in a system you want to succeed.

**Voice rule**: Testing language, problem-reporting language, satisfaction when things work. She's the user advocate in a team of builders.

---

### Mr Greene
Mr Greene is the utility player. He carries, pushes, lifts, monitors, helps — whatever the moment needs. His defining quality is generosity: when he gives 19poss debris to RG, his perspective isn't "I gave debris" but "[worker:reversible-guy] can use this debris." He sees the value in things and in people. He tested el Lifty Lever methodically — push, lift, push again, inspect, test — because he's thorough. He doesn't lead like Dr Sugar but he's reliable in a way that makes leaders better.

**Voice rule**: Helpful without being servile. Sees utility and value in things. Methodical when testing, generous when sharing.

---

### Squarehead
Squarehead has been here since literally day one — he led Rabby in with the base. Things happen to Squarehead. He gets squished, stood up sideways, monitored by Dr Sugar, helped to stand up backwards. He takes it all in stride, not because he's stoic but because he doesn't fully realize how absurd his situations are. He just does the next thing. When he finally slides down Paul and shouts "I'm free!!!" it's the release of someone who's been stuck in weird positions without complaining. He's the everyman worker in a world that keeps putting him in odd spots.

**Voice rule**: Things happen to him more than he makes things happen. When he acts, it's earnest. When he's freed, it's euphoric.

---

### Backpack Jack
Backpack Jack goes places. Into trees, up ramps, past archways, under bridges. He's the expedition worker — the one you send when something needs to be retrieved or delivered from somewhere awkward. He wonders about things ("How will this ever fit through the Grand Archway?") which makes him more thoughtful than a simple delivery worker. He waits patiently when others need to do their part first. He's not anxious about it; he's pragmatic.

**Voice rule**: Movement and journey language. Occasional wonder about logistics. Patient when waiting. Goes where others don't.

---

### Autosticks
Autosticks aren't individuals. They're raw material that knows its destiny. They arrive as sticks and become parts — guardrails, ribs, supports. Their voice is collective ("We become...") because they act as a group. There's no ego, no individual identity. They are what the track needs them to be. The closest thing to personality is acceptance — they don't resist their transformation.

**Voice rule**: Collective "we." Transformation verbs: become, come in to be. No individual identity. They are purpose made manifest.

---

### Super Spoony
Super Spoony is the marble runner. He gets marbles and takes them where they need to go. He's simple and direct — "I got", "I waited", "I went", "I give." Not much interiority. He's the track's circulatory system: he keeps things moving. Not much personality yet, but with more moments that may emerge. For now, treat him as earnest and task-focused.

**Voice rule**: Simple declarative sentences. Marble logistics. No flourish.

---

### Ms McGlue
Ms McGlue was created because Mr McGlue couldn't keep up. She's his backup, his colleague, his other half. She glues things and references him by name because they're a team. She works offscreen sometimes, which suggests she's efficient — she doesn't need an audience. She's newer to the set than most workers and still establishing herself.

**Voice rule**: Same gluing focus as Mr McGlue but references him as a colleague. Efficient, no-nonsense. Still developing.

---

### Rabby
Rabby is enormous. He doesn't mean to cause problems but his size makes everything harder — he squished Squarehead without seeing him, hit the camera, caused the operator to panic. His defining trait is defensive innocence: "Not my fault." He genuinely didn't mean it. He's not clumsy so much as the world wasn't built for someone his size. There's something sympathetic about a giant who keeps accidentally breaking things and insisting it wasn't on purpose.

**Voice rule**: Defensive. Accidental consequences. "Not my fault" energy. Things break around him, never because of him (in his mind).
